Short Put Strategy Example

The Short Put strategy allows traders to generate income by selling a put option and collecting the premium, with the potential obligation to buy the underlying asset if its price drops below the strike level. This strategy is particularly useful when you expect the asset’s price to remain stable or rise moderately. [cite: 26]

Let’s assume we are analyzing stock XZY, currently trading at $110. Instead of buying shares immediately, we sell one Put option contract (representing 100 shares) with: [cite: 27, 28]

  • Strike price (K): $95 [cite: 29]
  • Premium: $0.50 (or $50 total) [cite: 30]
  • Expiration: 1 week [cite: 31]

By selling this option, we immediately receive a premium of $50. This amount is our maximum potential profit from the trade. If, at expiration, the price of XZY remains above $95, the option expires worthless — we keep the premium and are not obligated to buy the stock. [cite: 32, 33, 34, 35]

If, instead, the stock falls below the strike price of $95, we will likely be assigned and required to purchase 100 shares at $95 each. However, since we collected a $0.50 premium, our effective purchase price becomes $94.5 per share. Below this level, the position starts to incur a loss. [cite: 36, 37, 38, 39]

The profit and loss structure of the position is summarized as follows: [cite: 40]

  • Maximum Profit: $50 (the premium collected) [cite: 41]
  • Break-even Price: $94.5 (strike − premium) [cite: 42]
  • Maximum Theoretical Loss: $9,450 (if XZY were to fall to $0) [cite: 43]

This strategy is often chosen by investors who are comfortable owning the underlying asset at a discount, since assignment results in buying the stock at a lower effective price while still earning income from the premium. [cite: 44, 45]

The chart above illustrates the Short Put payoff profile: profits remain constant above the strike price, while losses increase linearly below it. [cite: 52]

This approach can be repeated periodically to collect option premiums, provided the underlying’s fundamentals and volatility remain within acceptable risk levels. [cite: 53]

Covered Call Strategy with Stock

Let’s assume we hold 100 shares of stock XZY in our portfolio, purchased some time ago at $100 each. Today, the stock is worth $110. The cost basis (initial investment) is $10,000 (100 shares x $100), while the current market value is $11,000 (100 shares x $110). [cite: 56, 57]

This gives us an unrealized profit of $1,000 (10%). Suppose we don’t want to sell the stock just yet, but we’ve identified a price level at which we’d be satisfied to sell, locking in a 15% gain. [cite: 58]

Example — Selling the Call
Suppose we sell a Call option with a strike price of $115, expiring in one week, for a premium of $1. Since each contract covers 100 shares, the buyer of the Call immediately pays $100 (1 x 100 x $1) to us. [cite: 59, 60, 61, 62]

What happens at expiration, seven days later? If the price of XZY exceeds the strike price — for example, it rises to $117 — we are obligated to sell our shares at $115 to the buyer of the Call. (Important note: the buyer is not obligated to exercise the option, but since they hold a Call contract that allows them to purchase an asset worth $117 on the market for just $115, providing an immediate $2 profit, they will almost certainly choose to exercise it). [cite: 63, 64]

In this situation, we will be satisfied because we have achieved exactly what we planned for: selling the stock with a 15% gain. Of course, the seller forgoes a $2 profit compared to selling directly on the market at $117 (since they’re selling at the $115 strike price), but they’ve still reached their target profit: $15 per share from capital appreciation (15%) plus the $1 per share from selling the Call (1% premium), for a total gain of 16%. [cite: 66, 67]

If, instead, the price of the underlying stays below the strike price — say, it reaches $114 — nothing happens. The Call expires worthless. The buyer loses the $100 paid to the seller, who keeps the amount as profit, earning a 1% return on their investment, which consists of the 100 shares of XZY still held in the portfolio. These shares remain in the portfolio, and the same strategy can be repeated each week. [cite: 68, 69, 70]

Obviously, if our asset drops sharply in price — say, down to $90 — everything previously mentioned still applies (we still collect the $100), but we now have an unrealized loss of $10 per share. However, this has nothing to do with the sale of the Call, which still generated $100 in income — money we wouldn’t have earned without that transaction. [cite: 70, 71]

Also, this is a way to generate a relatively constant cash flow with the risk of having to sell the asset to the Call buyer, but still doing so at a profit. Thus, there’s no actual risk involved: there are no additional risks because the stock was already part of the portfolio; the Call seller wouldn’t have acted differently even if the asset’s price had fallen, since exit levels (such as a drop below a certain threshold) or other risk management measures like an evaluation of the asset’s fundamentals (which are considered strong in this case) had already been taken into account. [cite: 72, 73]

This operation can be repeated indefinitely, and that's exactly what many fund managers do with the stocks in their portfolio; it is a widely used method for generating cash without making any changes to the underlying holding. [cite: 74]

Another important aspect: if the stock "gets called away," as they say (meaning it is sold because it has reached or exceeded the strike price), that asset is removed from the portfolio. At that point, you have realized your investment, and XZY can simply be repurchased either directly on the market or at a discount by selling a Put. [cite: 75]

The maximum risk is described as "undefined" because it cannot be precisely quantified; in the event of a significant drop in the price of the underlying stock, the potential loss can be substantial. However, this risk was already inherent in the position held before selling the Call, that is, when the stock was in the portfolio without an associated Call. [cite: 76]

The decision to retain this asset in the portfolio was made following a thorough evaluation of the issuer's solidity and stability. Any price volatility was deemed temporary and normal market behavior; it was determined that, in such circumstances, there would be no need to liquidate the position at a loss, thus keeping the investment strategy unchanged. [cite: 77]

From a strictly mathematical perspective, the maximum risk is not technically infinite, but rather equal to the total loss of the value of the stock in the event the stock price drops to zero. This is an extremely rare scenario, especially for companies that are stable and fundamentally sound; in such an extreme case, the maximum loss would be the entire amount invested in the position, which is $10,000, calculated as 100 shares purchased at $100 each. [cite: 78, 79, 80]

Contrary to what is often inaccurately stated, commonly named "undefined risk" strategies — such as Short Puts, Short Calls, and Covered Calls, among others — do not imply technically infinite risk in every case; there is a theoretical cap on the maximum loss. Furthermore, this cap is based on the fact that an asset’s price cannot fall below zero. [cite: 81]

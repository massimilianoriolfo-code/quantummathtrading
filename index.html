Covered Call Strategy with Stock

Posted on November 10, 2025

Let’s assume we hold 100 shares of stock XZY in our portfolio, purchased at $100 each. Today, the stock is worth $110. The cost basis is $10,000, while the current market value is $11,000 — an unrealized profit of $1,000 (10%). We don’t want to sell XZY yet because we believe it could rise further, up to $115. So, we sell a Call option with a strike of $115, expiring in one week, for a $1 premium.

If, at expiration, the price of XZY exceeds $115 (say $117), we are obligated to sell XZY at $115. The buyer profits by $2 per share, while we achieve our 15% target gain, plus the $1 premium — a total return of 16%.

If the price remains below $115, the Call expires worthless. We keep the $100 premium as profit — a 1% return — while continuing to hold our shares. This can be repeated weekly or monthly.

The main advantage: steady income from stocks already owned. The trade-off: capped upside if the stock rallies beyond the strike. Still, it’s a disciplined way to make capital work efficiently.

As shown in the figure, the Covered Call strategy generates a smooth, capped profit line — combining the linear gain of long stock with a flat plateau beyond the strike price.

Even in case of a price drop, the Call premium acts as partial protection, offsetting small declines. The risk remains limited to the underlying stock's decline, not to the option itself.

This method can be repeated indefinitely — a popular approach among fund managers to generate consistent cash flow without altering portfolio composition.

Importantly, selling a Call on an existing position doesn’t introduce new risks: the asset is already owned. If the stock gets “called away” (sold at strike), it simply leaves the portfolio, locking in profit. The position can later be rebuilt by repurchasing the asset — or selling a Put to re-enter at a discount.

From a mathematical standpoint, the maximum theoretical loss equals the total stock value (if XZY goes to zero). In practice, such scenarios are extremely rare for solid companies. Hence, the Covered Call remains a conservative, income-focused strategy.

It’s also essential to compare Covered Calls with Short Puts. Although their P/L charts are almost identical, the key difference lies in capital allocation: in a Short Put, the collateral remains liquid and interest-bearing; in a Covered Call, the capital is invested in the stock, generating dividends but no interest on cash reserves.

Ultimately, both strategies share the same risk/reward profile — the choice depends on whether you prefer to hold the underlying stock or keep your capital in cash while collecting option premiums.

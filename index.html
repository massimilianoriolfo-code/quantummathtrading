Short Put Strategy Example

The Short Put strategy allows traders to generate income by selling a put option and collecting the premium, with the potential obligation to buy the underlying asset if its price drops below the strike level. This strategy is particularly useful when you expect the asset’s price to remain stable or rise moderately.

Let’s assume we are analyzing stock XZY, currently trading at $110. Instead of buying shares immediately, we sell one Put option contract (representing 100 shares) with: Strike price (K): $95, Premium: $0.50 (or $50 total), Expiration: 1 week.

By selling this option, we immediately receive a premium of $50. This amount is our maximum potential profit from the trade. If, at expiration, the price of XZY remains above $95, the option expires worthless — we keep the premium and are not obligated to buy the stock.

If, instead, the stock falls below the strike price of $95, we will likely be assigned and required to purchase 100 shares at $95 each. However, since we collected a $0.50 premium, our effective purchase price becomes $94.5 per share. Below this level, the position starts to incur a loss.

The profit and loss structure of the position is summarized as follows: Maximum Profit: $50, Break-even Price: $94.5, Maximum Theoretical Loss: $9,450 (if XZY were to fall to $0).

The chart above illustrates the Short Put payoff profile: profits remain constant above the strike price, while losses increase linearly below it.

Covered Call Strategy with Stock

Let’s assume we hold 100 shares of stock XZY in our portfolio, purchased some time ago at $100 each. Today, the stock is worth $110. The cost basis is $10,000, while the current market value is $11,000. This gives us an unrealized profit of $1,000 (10%).

Example — Selling the Call
So, we sell a Call option with a strike price of $115, expiring in one week, for a premium of $1. The buyer of the Call immediately pays $100 to us.

What happens at expiration? If the price exceeds the strike — for example, $117 — we sell at $115. We’ve reached our target: $15 per share capital appreciation plus $1 premium, total 16% gain. If it stays below $115, the Call expires worthless, we keep the $100 premium and the shares, and can repeat the strategy.

Contrary to what is often inaccurately stated, commonly referred “undefined risk” strategies — such as Short Puts and Covered Calls — do not imply technically infinite risk. The maximum loss corresponds to the stock price dropping to zero. This operation can be repeated indefinitely to generate cash flow.
